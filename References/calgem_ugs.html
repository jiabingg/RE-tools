🔶 What Is Underground Gas Storage?

In California, natural gas is stored underground in depleted oil or gas fields to maintain reliable supply for electrical power generation, industrial, residential, and commercial use. Gas is injected through storage wells and withdrawn when needed. CalGEM's UGS program ensures storage operations are conducted safely through comprehensive regulation.

Underground Gas Storage Project
A project for the injection and withdrawal of natural gas into an underground reservoir for storage purposes. Includes the storage reservoir, confining strata, gas storage wells, observation wells, wellheads, and attendant facilities and appurtenances.
Field Capacity
The total gas storage capacity, including both base (cushion) gas and working gas, of an underground gas storage facility, measured in cubic feet.
Working Gas vs. Base Gas
Base (cushion) gas is the permanent inventory needed to maintain adequate pressure and deliverability. Working gas is the volume above the cushion gas that is available to the marketplace for sale and delivery.
Deliverability
The amount of natural gas that can be withdrawn from a storage facility on a daily basis, usually expressed in millions of cubic feet per day (MMcf/d).

How Underground Gas Storage Works

🔷 Federal & State Dual Oversight

CalGEM regulates UGS under California law (CCR §1726–§1726.10, PRC §3180–§3187). PHMSA (Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration) provides federal oversight under 49 CFR §192.12. CalGEM has a §60106 agreement with PHMSA to act as a state partner, inspecting UGS facilities on PHMSA's behalf.

🟢 Key Regulatory Milestones

  • Oct 1, 2018: Comprehensive UGS regulations (CCR §1726–§1726.10) took effect — covering safety, risk management, monitoring, reporting, and testing for all gas storage operators.
  • SB 463 (2019): Directed CalGEM to address Aliso Canyon root causes and develop chemical inventory reporting requirements.
  • Jul 1, 2025: SB 463 chemical inventory and root cause analysis regulations took effect — requiring chemical tracking, expanded corrosion evaluation, and well-specific well control plans.
  • Jul 2022: CalGEM formalized its Unannounced Random Onsite Well (UROW) inspection program.

🔶 Regulatory Framework

Underground Gas Storage in California is governed by Article 5 of CCR Title 14, Chapter 4, Subchapter 1 — sections 1726 through 1726.10. These regulations are separate from the UIC program (Article 4) and set specific requirements for gas storage well construction, testing, monitoring, risk management, and emergency response.

🟣 SB 463 (Stern, 2019) — Root Cause & Chemical Inventory

Senate Bill 463 directed CalGEM to revise UGS regulations to address root causes identified in the Aliso Canyon 2015 well leak investigation and develop new chemical inventory reporting requirements. The resulting regulations (effective July 1, 2025) require:

  • Operators must track chemicals that could be emitted from a UGS well during a reportable leak
  • Expanded corrosion evaluation, mitigation, and monitoring requirements
  • Well-specific well control plans for each gas storage well
⚠ Separate from UIC: Underground Gas Storage regulations (CCR §1726–§1726.10) are Article 5 — distinct from the Underground Injection Control program (CCR §1724.5–§1724.13, Article 4). Different well types, different requirements.

🔴 Core Design Principle: No Single Point of Failure

UGS regulations require that gas storage wells are constructed to ensure no single point of failure. Injected gas must be confined to the approved storage reservoir at all times. This is achieved through dual mechanical barrier design as specified in §1726.5.

🔶 Well Construction Requirements — §1726.5

  • Dual Barrier Design: Wells must have at least two independent mechanical barriers between the stored gas and the surface/underground sources of drinking water
  • Primary Barrier: Production casing and tubing with packer — injection/withdrawal through tubing only (no casing-head injection)
  • Secondary Barrier: Additional casing string(s) with cement providing redundant containment
  • Cement Requirements: Cement must isolate the storage zone and protect all underground sources of drinking water
  • Alternative Methods: Operators may propose alternatives but must demonstrate they meet the performance standards of the regulation
⚠ Critical: No injection/withdrawal method other than through tubing with packer (primary mechanical barrier per §1726.5(b)(1)(A)) is permitted by UGS regulations.

🔷 Mechanical Integrity Testing — §1726.6

Gas storage wells must undergo regular mechanical integrity testing to verify well construction integrity:

  • Pressure testing of casing and tubing at required intervals
  • Verification of packer integrity
  • Corrosion evaluation and monitoring (expanded under SB 463 regulations)
  • Logging and diagnostic testing as required by the Division

🔵 Area of Review — §1726.4

Operators must evaluate all wells within the Area of Review (AoR) for their UGS project:

  • All wells completed in or penetrating through the storage reservoir must be identified and evaluated for containment assurance
  • Wells in the same or deeper zone require casing diagrams and flat file data (§1726.4.1)
  • Wells in shallower zones must also be identified with total depth and estimated top of storage reservoir
  • Evaluation must consider design of storage operation volumes, pressures, and flow rates

🔶 Risk Management Plans (RMP) — §1726.3

Every UGS operator must develop and maintain a Risk Management Plan for each storage project. RMPs evaluate threats and hazards to safety, health, and the environment and must identify prevention and mitigation protocols that effectively address those threats.

1

Threat & Hazard Identification

Systematically identify all threats and hazards associated with project operations, including well integrity, reservoir containment, surface facilities, and natural hazards.

2

Risk Assessment

Evaluate the likelihood and consequence of each identified threat/hazard. Consider well age, construction, geological conditions, and operational history.

3

Prevention & Mitigation Protocols — §1726.3.1

Develop specific protocols to prevent and mitigate each identified risk. Must consider whether subsurface safety valves are appropriate. Risk-based, site-specific approach required.

4

Implementation & Documentation

Implement all protocols and maintain documentation. Plans must be updated immediately as conditions change.

5

Ongoing Review & Update

RMPs must be reviewed and updated regularly. CalGEM reviews RMP submissions and may require modifications.

🔷 Monitoring Requirements — §1726.7

  • Continuous Monitoring: Wellhead pressure, annular pressure, and flow rate monitoring
  • Periodic Monitoring: Reservoir pressure surveys, gas composition testing, subsurface monitoring
  • Surface Monitoring: Gas leak detection using FLIR cameras and other methods
  • Corrosion Monitoring: Expanded under SB 463 — comprehensive corrosion evaluation program

🔴 Emergency Response Plans (ERP) — §1726.8

Each UGS project must have an Emergency Response Plan covering any and all leaks that require response — from small anomalies to major incidents. ERPs must include:

  • Notification procedures for CalGEM and other agencies
  • Response actions for different leak scenarios
  • Key personnel and contact information
  • Coordination with local emergency services
⚠ ERP Triennial Review: Emergency Response Plans must be reviewed at least every 3 years statewide. ERPs must also be updated when key personnel change, and are reviewed as part of RMP updates.

🟣 Reportable Leaks — §1726.9

Section 1726.9 defines leaks that require full-scale response from CalGEM and other regulatory agencies due to potential severity. These are distinct from the smaller leaks addressed under the ERP — reportable leaks demand "all hands on deck" response.

🟢 SB 463 Chemical Inventory (Eff. July 1, 2025)

Operators must now maintain an inventory of chemicals that could potentially be emitted from a UGS well during a reportable leak. This addresses recommendations from the Aliso Canyon root cause investigation and helps emergency responders understand potential exposure risks.

🔴 Aliso Canyon — Largest CA Storage Facility

The Aliso Canyon facility in Los Angeles County is California's largest UGS facility, with a capacity of over 86 billion cubic feet. Converted from oil production to gas storage in the early 1970s by SoCalGas, it was the site of the massive 2015 well blowout (SS-25) that displaced thousands of residents. This event drove major regulatory reform including the 2018 UGS regulations and SB 463.

🔶 UROW Inspection Program

Pursuant to PRC §3185, CalGEM is required to perform Unannounced Random Onsite Well (UROW) inspections of some gas storage wells annually. CalGEM formalized and updated this program in July 2022.

🔷 What UROW Inspections Cover

During a UROW inspection, a CalGEM field engineer visits the randomly selected well site and performs:

  • Environmental Well Inspection: Visual inspection of wellhead, cellar, and surrounding area
  • Gas Leak Detection Inspection: FLIR (Forward Looking InfraRed) camera survey for gas leaks at surface
  • Well Sign Check: Per CCR §1722.1.1(a) — visible, legible, permanently affixed sign with operator name, lease name/number, and well number
  • Waste/Refuse Check: Per CCR §1775(a) — no oilfield waste disposed in manner causing damage to life, health, property, or water resources
1

Random Well Selection

CalGEM selects approximately 25% of all gas storage wells statewide at random for the year's inspection program.

2

Unannounced Arrival

CalGEM field engineers arrive at UGS fields without advance warning. The well list is not shared with operators.

3

On-Site Inspection

Environmental well inspection and gas leak detection using FLIR cameras are conducted on the selected wells.

4

Findings Documented

Results are documented. Any violations or concerns are addressed through CalGEM's enforcement process.

5

Annual Report Published

CalGEM publishes annual UROW inspection reports summarizing the program and findings.

✅ UROW is Additive: The UROW program is an addition to, and does not replace, CalGEM's other routine inspections, facility oversight, well test witnessing, and regulatory oversight activities.

🟢 CalGEM's Broader UGS Oversight

Beyond UROW inspections, CalGEM's ongoing UGS oversight includes:

  • Reviewing and approving Risk Management Plans
  • Witnessing well tests and integrity evaluations
  • Performing periodic facility inspections
  • Permitting and overseeing well work activities
  • Reviewing production/injection data and records
  • Inspecting wells and facilities for potential hazards and leaks
  • Reviewing abandonment operations

🟣 PHMSA Federal Inspections

CalGEM also acts as PHMSA's state partner under a §60106 agreement, inspecting UGS facilities on behalf of the federal agency. Enforcement letters resulting from CalGEM inspections are issued by PHMSA and published on their stakeholder outreach website.
