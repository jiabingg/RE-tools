The Underground Injection Control (UIC) Program

A Technical Guide for Engineers on Regulation, Review, and Compliance

The Core Mandate: Protect All Protected Waters

The entire CalGEM UIC program is built on one non-negotiable principle: the absolute protection of all current and future sources of drinking water. Every regulation, every data request, and every review your project undergoes is designed to verify and guarantee this protection. As the stamping engineer, you are the ultimate guarantor that the project design prevents fluid migration into Underground Sources of Drinking Water (USDWs).

10,000
mg/L Total Dissolved Solids

This is the threshold. Any formation containing water with less than this concentration is a protected USDW by default, unless proven otherwise through an Aquifer Exemption.

The Regulatory Pillars of the UIC Program

California Public Resources Code (PRC) & Code of Regulations (CCR)

These are the state-level statutes and rules that give CalGEM its authority. For UIC, the most critical sections govern well construction standards, mechanical integrity testing (MIT) requirements, and Area of Review (AOR) determinations. You must demonstrate that your project's cementing, casing, and monitoring plans meet or exceed the standards set forth in Title 14 of the CCR. Any deviation requires a specific, technically justified variance request.

Federal Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA)

The federal SDWA establishes the nationwide framework for protecting groundwater. CalGEM's UIC program must be at least as stringent as the EPA's requirements. This is why the 10,000 mg/L TDS standard is so rigid‚Äîit's a federal baseline. The SDWA mandates that no injection activity can be authorized that "allows the movement of fluid containing any contaminant into underground sources of drinking water, if the presence of that contaminant may cause a violation of any primary drinking water regulation... or may otherwise adversely affect the health of persons."

Aquifer Exemption (AE) Regulations

This is the regulatory mechanism that allows injection into a formation that would otherwise be a USDW. An AE does not mean the water is not protected; it means the aquifer has been formally deemed unsuitable as a drinking water source. To receive an AE, the operator must prove to CalGEM and the EPA that the aquifer does not currently serve as a source of drinking water and cannot in the future because it is either economically or technologically impractical to render that water fit for human consumption, or it is hydrocarbon-producing. Your projects must operate strictly within the defined lateral and vertical boundaries of an approved AE.

Ongoing Compliance & Enforcement

Project approval is not the finish line. Continuous monitoring, testing, and reporting are required to maintain compliance. Failure in these areas is the primary source of UIC-related enforcement actions.
